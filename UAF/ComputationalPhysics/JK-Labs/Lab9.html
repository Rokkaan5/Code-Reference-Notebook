Lab 9: Wave Motion

Author

Jasmine Kobayashi

  • We have found similarities between oscillatory phenomena and wave motion.

  • For a system of \(N\) coupled oscillators, the individual oscillation of particles leads to a propagation of energy over arbitrary distances.

  • Difference between waves and oscillatory motion of coupled systems is one of scale.

  • Consider the longitudinal oscillations of a linear chain of \(N\) particles with identical spring constants \(k\) and equal equilibrium separation \(a\). The equation of motion is \[\begin{equation*} \frac{d^2 u_i}{dt^2} = -\frac{k}{m} (2 u_i - u_{i+1} - u_{i-1}) \end{equation*}\] where we consider the limits \(N \rightarrow \infty\), \(a \rightarrow 0\).

  • It can be shown that the discrete equations of motion can be replaced by the continuous wave equation \[\begin{equation*} \boxed{ \frac{\partial^2 u(x,t)}{\partial t^2} = {v^2}\frac{\partial^2 u(x,t)}{\partial x^2}} \end{equation*}\] where \(v\) has dimensions of velocity.

  • The wave equation can be obtained from the discrete equation of motion as follows. Replace \(u_i(t)\) by \(u(x,t)\), where \(x\) is a continuous variable. \[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial^2 u(x,t)}{\partial t^2} &= \frac{k a^2}{m}\frac{1}{a^2} \left[ u(x+a,t) - 2u(x,t) + u(x-a,t)\right]\\ &= \frac{T}{\mu} \left[ u(x+a,t) - 2u(x,t) + u(x-a,t)\right]\\ \end{align*}\] where \(\mu = m/a\) (mass density) and \(T = k/a\) (tension force on the continuous ``string’’), with \(v^2 = T/\mu\).

  • Note that the time derivative is written as a partial derivative because \(u(x,t)\) is a function of two variables.

  • Now use a Taylor series expansion

\[\begin{align*} u(x + a) &= u(x) +\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} (a)+ \frac{1}{2!} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}(a^2) + \cdot \cdot \cdot\\ u(x - a) &= u(x) -\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} (a)+ \frac{1}{2!} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}(a^2) + \cdot \cdot \cdot\\ \end{align*}\]

\[\begin{align*} \implies u(x+a) + u(x-a) = 2u(x) + \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}(a^2) + \cdot \cdot \cdot \end{align*}\]

or

\[\begin{align*} \boxed{ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \approx \frac{1}{a^2} \left[ u(x+a)- 2u(x)+ u(x-a) \right]} \end{align*}\]

as \(a \rightarrow 0\). - And the wave equation for the continuous ``string’’ is

\[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial^2 u(x,t)}{\partial t^2} &= \left(\frac{T}{\mu} \right) \\ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} &= v^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \end{align*}\]

  • Traveling waves are solutions to the wave equation. These are functions of the form \(f(x \pm vt)\).
  • Examples of solutions (try it!) to the wave equation are

\[\begin{align*} u(x,t) &=& A \cos \frac{2 \pi}{\lambda}(x \pm vt)\\ u(x,t) &=& A \sin \frac{2 \pi}{\lambda}(x \pm vt)\\ \end{align*}\] where \(\lambda\) is the wavelength and \(v\) is the wave speed. - Since the wave equation is a linear equation and hence satisfies a superposition principle, we can understand the behavior of a wave of arbitrary shape by using Fourier’s theorem and represent its shape as a sum of sinusoidal waves.

Exercise 3: Dispersive waves

Nondispersive waves retain their shape as time is increased. Such a wave is said to be nondispersive because of the linear relation between \(\omega\) and \(k\), this is, each harmonic of the wave moves with the same speed. If the speed of propagation depends on wavelength, the wave is said to be dispersive and the form of the wave changes with time.

Exercise 4: Numerical solutions of the wave equation

  • The wave equation is

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} =v^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \end{equation*}\]

  • Numerical solutions can be obtained by replacing the second derivative on the right-hand side with the a discrete difference,

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} \left [ u(x + \Delta x, t) - 2 u(x,t) + u(x - \Delta x, t) \right ] \end{equation*}\]

  • Thus, we can write a second-order ordinary differential equation for each grid point,

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d^2 u}{dt^2} = \frac{v^2}{\Delta x^2} \left [ u(x + \Delta x, t) - 2 u(x,t) + u(x - \Delta x, t) \right ] \end{equation*}\]

  • Now change the second-order ordinary differential equation into two first-order equations by defining a new variable \(\psi\),

\[\begin{align*} \frac{du}{dt} &=& \psi(x,t)\\ \frac{d \psi}{dt} & = & \frac{v^2}{\Delta x^2} \left [ u(x + \Delta x, t) - 2 u(x,t) + u(x - \Delta x, t) \right ] \end{align*}\]

  • Applying a first-order Euler scheme, these equations become two Forward Time Centered Space (FTCS) equations.
  • FTCS Scheme: two-point forward difference approximation for the time derivative and a three-point centered difference approximation for the spatial derivative. Subscripts now refer to spatial grid index.

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\psi_j^{n+1} - \psi_j^n}{\Delta t} - \frac{v^2(u_{j-1}^n - 2 u_j^n + u_{j+1}^n)}{\Delta x^2} = 0 \end{equation*}\]

or

\[\begin{equation*} \boxed{\psi_j^{n+1} = \psi_j^n + s \left( u_{j-1}^n + 2 u_j^n + u_{j+1}^n\right )} \end{equation*}\]

where $ s = v^2 t/x^2$.

def initial_wave(x,x0,A,h):
    u = A*np.exp(-(x-x0)**2/h**2)          #u = A*e^(-(x-x0)^2/h^2
    psi = np.zeros(len(x))                 #array of length x of zeros

def solve_wave(u,psi,tfinal):
    t = 0
    uarr = []
    tarr = []
    psip = psi
    while t < tfinal:                      #np.roll goes from the last position and "rolls" back to the first
        psip[:] = psi[:] + s*(np.roll(u[:],1) - 2*u[:] + np.roll(u[:],-1))  #psi^n+1 = psi^n + s(u_(j-1) - 2u_j + u_(j+1))
        u = u + dt*psi                     #u = u + dt*psi
        psi = psip
        uarr.append(u)
        tarr.append(t)
        t += dt
    return uarr,tarr
